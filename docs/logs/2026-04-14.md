## Background and context for this request

I am a researcher working on a project to speed up **tree-based speculative decoding** for
large language models. My goal is to find papers that **clash with, pre-empt, or
undermine** a specific research idea I am about to describe. I already have a small
seed set of papers I'm aware of; I need you to sweep the literature beyond that seed

### What speculative decoding is (for grounding)

Speculative decoding accelerates inference for a large target LLM by pairing it with
a small draft model. Each cycle, the draft model guesses several future tokens, the
target model verifies all the guesses in a single parallel forward pass, and the
target accepts the longest matching prefix before emitting a correction at the first
mismatch. **Tree-based** variants generalize this: the draft model builds a *tree*
of candidate continuations (multiple paths in parallel) rather than a single linear
sequence, and the target verifies the tree with a tree-attention mask.

Two hyperparameters dominate the speed/quality tradeoff in tree-based methods:
- **Draft depth `D`**: how many layers deep the draft tree grows
- **Verification size `V`**: how many of the tree's candidate tokens are sent to
  the target model for verification

The target optimization metric is **throughput** (accepted tokens ÷ total wall-clock
cycle time), not acceptance length alone, because overbuilding the tree costs more
time than it saves.

### The baseline we build on: Learning to Draft (LTD)

Zhang et al., **"Learning to Draft: Adaptive Speculative Decoding with Reinforcement
Learning"** (arXiv:2603.01639, 2026). Briefly:

- Sits on top of Eagle3 as a learned controller.
- Trains **two separate MLP policies** with PPO:
  - **Depth policy** (1-layer MLP, 1024 hidden): called after every draft-layer
    expansion; emits binary CONTINUE/STOP; observation is the current frontier's
    log-probs plus scalar depth and sequence length; discount γ=0.999 (high, because
    reward is terminal).
  - **Size policy** (2-layer MLP, [1024, 256]): called once after the tree is fully
    built; emits a discrete action mapping to `V ∈ {20, 40, ..., 240}`; observation
    is all tree log-probs plus final depth and sequence length; discount γ=0.9.
- Reward signal is cycle throughput: `L_A / (T_draft + T_verify)`.
- Training curriculum: (1) train size policy against random draft depths sampled
  uniformly from [1, 12], (2) train depth policy against fixed `V = 60`, (3)
  alternate to co-adapt. Two alternation rounds suffice per the paper.
- Results: 2.24×–4.32× speedup over vanilla AR decoding; up to +36.4% over Eagle3;
  under 1.5% policy overhead on Llama.

### Our idea to beat LTD (idea3 — the latest version)

**Core claim:** LTD's two-policy split and its post-hoc width pruning are both
suboptimal. We can do better with a **single, lightweight MLP policy** that jointly
controls depth and width **during** tree construction.

**Architecture.** At every draft layer `m`, our MLP reads a richer state vector and
outputs a *tuple* `(f_m, c_m)` — two real numbers in `[0, 1]`:

- **`f_m` (frontier retention ratio)** — decides *which frontier parents to expand*
  for this layer. Given the current frontier with cumulative path probabilities
  `s_1, ..., s_n`, we normalize them into a distribution over the frontier, sort
  descending by weight, and select the **smallest top-k set whose cumulative weight
  is at least `f_m`**. Only those frontier parents get expanded. This is
  nucleus-sampling–style cumulative-mass truncation, but applied to the *frontier*
  rather than to a single token distribution, and governed by a learned policy
  rather than a fixed threshold.
- **`c_m` (child-mass retention ratio)** — decides, for each expanded parent,
  *how many of its children to keep*. Rank the parent's candidate children by next-
  token probability; keep the smallest top-k set with cumulative probability mass
  ≥ `c_m`.

This gives us dynamic **width pruning during construction** (both across the frontier
and within each parent's children), not just after. When `f_m` is small, fewer paths
are expanded that layer, saving drafter forward passes. When `c_m` is small, each
expansion stays narrow. When both are near 1, the method reduces to a standard
beam search.

**Depth control is implicit.** Because `f_m` can select zero frontier nodes in the
limit (or because we can add a simple "stop if no expanded frontier" rule), we don't
need a separate depth policy. Growth halts naturally when the policy decides there
are no promising frontier paths left to extend.

**State vector.** Richer than LTD's: current draft depth, current context length,
budget/ratios allocated at previous layers, cumulative log-probabilities of frontier
paths, and next-token probability distributions. We will experiment with both MLP
and SSM policy heads (to see whether a sequential model over layers buys anything
over the per-layer MLP).

**Reward.** LTD uses `accepted_tokens / (T_draft + T_verify)`. We use
`accepted_tokens / T_total`, where `T_total` includes policy inference time. This
pushes the optimizer to care about the cost of its own calls, which is nontrivial
when the policy is wider (e.g., SSM) or called more often.

**Training.** LTD uses pure PPO (on-policy, sample-inefficient, required 1M depth-
policy steps and 100K size-policy steps). We plan to ablate three alternatives:

1. **Pure PPO** (LTD's recipe) — the baseline for fair comparison.
2. **Oracle-labeled imitation learning / DAgger.** Construct an oracle labeler by
   grid-searching or exhaustively evaluating `(f_m, c_m)` choices offline on cached
   draft trees; train the policy via supervised regression on the oracle's labels,
   then optionally DAgger-style correct distribution shift by rolling out the
   learned policy and relabeling.
3. **Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) with a continuous action.** Unlike LTD's discrete
   action space, our `(f_m, c_m)` is naturally continuous in `[0, 1]²`, which fits
   SAC's entropy-regularized continuous control regime. SAC is off-policy → far
   more sample-efficient than PPO.

**Expected wins over LTD:**

1. *Less wasted drafter compute* — we prune width *during* construction, not just
   at verification time.
2. *Smaller total policy footprint* — one MLP instead of two.
3. *Natural continuous-control formulation* — opens up off-policy methods that PPO
   forecloses on.
4. *Richer observation* — captures information LTD's depth-policy ablations
   suggested was missing (cumulative log-probs, allocation history, full next-token
   distributions).

**Plan for evaluation.** Reproduce LTD on Modal with Qwen3-14B and Qwen3-32B as
targets and their Eagle3 heads as drafters. Train on HumanEval; evaluate on
MT-Bench, GSM8K, Alpaca, and Natural Questions. Report end-to-end speedup vs.
vanilla AR and vs. LTD, plus average acceptance length τ per cycle.

Literature sweep: threats to the joint tree-width controller for speculative decoding

**Idea3's core claim — that a single MLP outputting continuous (f_m, c_m) retention ratios can jointly control tree depth and width during draft construction — faces no exact prior-art duplicate, but confronts serious threats from at least three directions.** C2T already deploys a learned classifier for per-node tree pruning during construction, directly pre-empting the "learned dynamic width" component. SAC's well-documented gradient saturation in bounded [0,1]² action spaces and the LLM inference community's obsession with eliminating even microsecond-level kernel launch overhead pose critical implementation risks. On the other hand, the single-policy joint-control design finds strong support in the co-adaptation literature: unified RL consistently outperforms modular alternatives when objectives align. Below are **38 papers** across five buckets, ordered by severity within each bucket.

## Bucket A — Direct prior art on the core mechanism

These papers already implement some form of learned or dynamic tree-width control during draft construction. No paper replicates idea3's exact (f_m, c_m) dual-continuous-ratio mechanism, but two come close enough to qualify as high-severity prior art.

**1. C2T: A Classifier-Based Tree Construction Method in Speculative Decoding** Feiye Huo, Jianchao Tan, Kefeng Zhang, Xunliang Cai, Shengli Sun · arXiv:2502.13652 [Aussieai](https://www.aussieai.com/research/speculative-decoding) · Feb 2025 (submitted to ICLR 2026, withdrawn) **Bucket: A** · **Severity: Critical**

C2T trains a **2-layer FFN classifier with only 241 parameters** that takes three features (joint probability, entropy, depth) and outputs a confidence score per draft token, deciding whether to include it in the verification tree. [OpenReview](https://openreview.net/forum?id=8pX8He3Yyg) The classifier operates during tree construction, dynamically generating and pruning token trees layer by layer. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.13652) It reduces candidate tokens by **25%** while maintaining or improving acceptance length over EAGLE-2. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13652)[arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.13652) The classifier is plug-and-play on top of EAGLE-2/3 and demonstrates cross-dataset transferability. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.13652)

**Clash with idea3:** C2T is the closest existing work to idea3's core mechanism — a learned controller making per-node expansion decisions during tree construction. However, C2T uses **binary include/exclude** classification rather than continuous (f_m, c_m) ratios, employs only 3 fixed features (vs. idea3's richer state vector), and does not use nucleus-style cumulative-mass truncation. Idea3's continuous control and richer state representation are the primary differentiators. Challenges: **frontier retention concept, dynamic width during construction, learned controller design**.

**Artifact:** Code not yet publicly released.

**2. Neural Dynamic Expansion (NDE) for Multi-Path Speculative Decoding** Authors not fully specified · arXiv:2602.16994 · Feb 2026 **Bucket: A** · **Severity: High**

NDE uses a **neural network** trained with offline block-efficiency supervision to decide which nodes to expand during tree construction. Rather than expanding all nodes or using simple confidence thresholds, NDE produces context-dependent tree topologies by learning that aggressive early branching wastes compute. The paper also systematically analyzes verification algorithms (traversal vs. optimal-transport-based).

**Clash with idea3:** NDE uses a learned neural controller for dynamic during-construction tree expansion — the same architectural goal as idea3. The training signal (block efficiency) differs from idea3's RL-based reward, and the parameterization likely differs from continuous [0,1] retention ratios, but the conceptual overlap is substantial. Challenges: **frontier retention, dynamic width during construction, learned controller**.

**Artifact:** Not found.

**3. EAGLE-2: Faster Inference of Language Models with Dynamic Draft Trees** Yuhui Li, Fangyun Wei, Chao Zhang, Hongyang Zhang · EMNLP 2024 · arXiv:2406.16858 **Bucket: A** · **Severity: High**

EAGLE-2 introduces context-dependent dynamic draft trees by leveraging the observation that its draft model is well-calibrated: confidence scores approximate acceptance rates. **Clash with idea3:** EAGLE-2 performs dynamic width during construction using confidence as a proxy — conceptually similar to idea3's f_m. ## Bucket C — Findings that undermine key premises

These papers challenge idea3's assumptions. Organized by the premise attacked, with the two most critical vulnerabilities highlighted first.

### SAC gradient saturation in bounded action spaces (attacks Premise 4)

**18. Beyond Distributions: Geometric Action Control for Continuous RL (GAC)** Zhihao Lin et al. · arXiv:2511.08234 · Nov 2025 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: Critical**

GAC demonstrates a **fundamental geometric mismatch** between Gaussian distributions (used by SAC) and bounded action spaces. Analysis of trained SAC policies shows that **46.4% of pre-squashed samples have gradients below 0.1**, indicating severe gradient saturation at tanh boundaries. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.08234) GAC achieves **37.6% improvement** over SAC by using spherical normalization to avoid the Gaussian-tanh paradigm entirely. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.08234)

**Clash with idea3:** Idea3 proposes SAC with tanh-squashed outputs in [0,1]². This paper shows the exact mechanism SAC uses to bound actions causes severe gradient saturation in nearly half of all samples. The 2D bounded space is precisely where this problem is worst. Challenges: **continuous action space, SAC training**.

---

**19. Corrected Soft Actor Critic for Continuous Control** Yanjun Chen et al. · arXiv:2410.16739 · Oct 2024 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

The tanh transformation in SAC introduces "sampling errors" that are "particularly detrimental in tasks that require precise action control," leading to suboptimal performance and slower convergence. Proposes computing likelihood within the transformed bounded space.

**Clash with idea3:** Idea3's [0,1]² action space requires precise control of retention ratios. SAC's inherent sampling distortion directly impairs learning quality. Challenges: **continuous action space, SAC training**.

**20. Action Decoupled SAC (AD-SAC)** Yahao Xu et al. · Neurocomputing, 2023 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

Proposes decoupling hybrid action spaces into separate agents, each with its own critic and actor. Shows better convergence and robustness than single-agent approaches when actions have different semantics.

**Clash with idea3:** Directly proposes the architectural change idea3 avoids — decomposing into separate agents for different action types. Challenges: **single-MLP design, SAC training, continuous action space**.

### Policy call overhead (attacks Premise 5)

**21. Look Ma, No Bubbles! Sub-1ms Forward Pass for Llama-1B** Hazy Research (Stanford) · Blog, 2025 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: Critical**

Achieves **sub-1ms forward pass** for Llama-1B by eliminating all kernel launch overhead. Reports that **250μs** of the ~530μs forward pass is overhead (activation storage, consistency). [Hazy Research](https://hazyresearch.stanford.edu/blog/2025-05-27-no-bubbles) When the entire forward pass of a 1B model takes ~530μs, calling a policy MLP at every layer (16+ times) at even 10–20μs per call adds 160–320μs — **30–60% overhead on the draft model's forward pass**.

**Clash with idea3:** Puts overhead numbers in devastating context. Policy MLP calls at every layer would be a substantial fraction of the total inference budget. Challenges: **T_total reward, single-MLP design called per-layer**.

**22. Host Overhead Is Killing Your Inference Efficiency** Modal team · Blog, 2025 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

Reports that kernel launches take microseconds that "add up, especially for small models on small inputs." Shaving **30μs** of host overhead cut **20%** off end-to-end latency. [Modal](https://modal.com/blog/host-overhead-inference-efficiency) The team describes their mission as "spending hours shaving a microsecond off a code path." [Modal](https://modal.com/blog/host-overhead-inference-efficiency)

**Clash with idea3:** If idea3's MLP is called at every layer, each call requires a GPU kernel launch. Even sub-μs launches compound. Challenges: **policy call overhead, T_total reward**.

**23. MPK: Compiling LLMs into a MegaKernel** Xinhao Cheng, Zhihao Jia et al. · 2025 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

MPK eliminates kernel launch overhead by fusing everything into a single megakernel. Reduces per-token latency from **14.5ms to 12.5ms** (14% improvement) on A100 by eliminating overhead alone. [Medium](https://zhihaojia.medium.com/compiling-llms-into-a-megakernel-a-path-to-low-latency-inference-cf7840913c17) Adding a policy MLP requiring additional kernel launches goes against this entire trend.

**Clash with idea3:** The inference optimization community considers kernel launch overhead a primary bottleneck. Challenges: **policy call overhead**.

**24. Characterizing CPU-Induced Slowdowns in Multi-GPU LLM Inference** arXiv:2603.22774 · 2025 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

Under high CPU load, kernel launch latency can escalate from **microseconds to milliseconds**. Critically, **CUDA Graphs cannot capture dynamic scheduling decisions** at each decode step, "leaving CPU-side bottlenecks as a persistent limiting factor." [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.22774)

**Clash with idea3:** Idea3's per-layer policy decisions are inherently dynamic and cannot be optimized away with CUDA Graphs. Each call incurs full kernel launch overhead. Challenges: **policy call overhead, dynamic per-layer decisions**.

### Modular policies outperform unified ones (attacks Premise 2)

**25. Modular RL on Mujoban (Sense-Plan-Act)** DeepMind · ~2022 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

A modular RL architecture following a sense-plan-act hierarchy improves success from **9.4% to 78.7%** compared to a monolithic ResNet+LSTM baseline — nearly an order of magnitude. Each module has a prescribed role, enabling effective per-component training. [Google Sites](https://sites.google.com/view/modular-rl/)

**Clash with idea3:** When a task has distinct functional requirements (as depth vs. width decisions arguably have), modular decomposition can dramatically outperform monolithic approaches. Challenges: **single-MLP design**.

**26. Factored Action Spaces in Deep Reinforcement Learning (FARL)** Thomas Pierrot et al. · OpenReview (ICLR submission) **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

Shows that splitting action spaces into smaller components and choosing actions independently yields "astonishing results" in both PPO and SAC. ### Offline/imitation learning gap (attacks Premise 3)

**27. The Generalization Gap in Offline Reinforcement Learning** Mediratta et al. · ICLR 2024 · arXiv:2312.05742 **Bucket: C** · **Severity: High**

## Integrated threat assessment

The evidence organizes into three tiers of risk:

**Tier 1 — Implementation risks that could kill the project.** SAC's gradient saturation in bounded [0,1]² (GAC paper shows **46.4%** of samples affected) and per-layer policy overhead (when draft model forward passes are ~530μs, adding 160–320μs of policy calls is **30–60%** overhead) are the most dangerous threats. The mitigation path for SAC is clear: use GAC's geometric approach, PPO with clipped ratios, or Beta-distribution policies instead of Gaussian-tanh. For overhead, consider amortization (call the policy once per tree, not per layer) or fuse the MLP into the draft model's computation graph.

**Tier 2 — Prior art that narrows the novelty claim.** C2T and NDE already deploy learned controllers for dynamic tree construction. EAGLE-2, DySpec, TALON, and HeteroSpec demonstrate heuristic dynamic width. Idea3's specific novelty reduces to: (a) dual continuous [0,1] outputs vs. binary classification, (b) nucleus-style cumulative-mass truncation vs. threshold pruning, and (c) richer state representation. This narrowing requires idea3 to **demonstrate empirically** that continuous control and richer state outperform C2T's 241-parameter binary classifier — a high bar given C2T's simplicity.

**Tier 3 — The single-MLP design is well-supported.** Unlike the other threats, the co-adaptation literature consistently favors idea3's unified approach over LTD's modular design. Terry et al.'s theoretical result (centralization eliminates nonstationarity), CoRL's empirical finding (unified RL beats alternating), and the FARL analysis (factoring correlated actions introduces bias) all support a single MLP. However, the FARL paper also notes that independent factoring works well when dimensions are independent — if f_m and c_m turn out to be weakly correlated, LTD's split may be equivalent.

The strongest overall recommendation: idea3 should (1) replace SAC with PPO or a geometric alternative for the [0,1]² space, (2) benchmark aggressively against C2T and DySpec as the closest competitors, (3) address the overhead concern by profiling per-layer MLP cost against amortized alternatives, and (4) use CALM's conformal calibration framework to provide acceptance-rate guarantees for the continuous thresholds.
