## Background and context for this request

I am a researcher working on a project to speed up **tree-based speculative decoding** for
large language models. My goal is to find papers that **clash with, pre-empt, or
undermine** a specific research idea I am about to describe. I already have a small

### What speculative decoding is (for grounding)

Speculative decoding accelerates inference for a large target LLM by pairing it with
a small draft model. Each cycle, the draft model guesses several future tokens, the
target model verifies all the guesses in a single parallel forward pass, and the
target accepts the longest matching prefix before emitting a correction at the first
mismatch. **Tree-based** variants generalize this: the draft model builds a *tree*
of candidate continuations (multiple paths in parallel) rather than a single linear
sequence, and the target verifies the tree with a tree-attention mask.

Two hyperparameters dominate the speed/quality tradeoff in tree-based methods:

- **Draft depth `D`**: how many layers deep the draft tree grows
- **Verification size `V`**: how many of the tree's candidate tokens are sent to
the target model for verification

The target optimization metric is **throughput** (accepted tokens ÷ total wall-clock
cycle time), not acceptance length alone, because overbuilding the tree costs more
time than it saves.

### The baseline we build on: Learning to Draft (LTD)

Zhang et al., **"Learning to Draft: Adaptive Speculative Decoding with Reinforcement
Learning"** (arXiv:2603.01639, 2026). Briefly:

- Sits on top of Eagle3 as a learned controller.
- Trains **two separate MLP policies** with PPO:
  - **Depth policy** (1-layer MLP, 1024 hidden): called after every draft-layer
  expansion; emits binary CONTINUE/STOP; observation is the current frontier's
  log-probs plus scalar depth and sequence length; discount γ=0.999 (high, because
  reward is terminal).
  - **Size policy** (2-layer MLP, [1024, 256]): called once after the tree is fully
  built; emits a discrete action mapping to `V ∈ {20, 40, ..., 240}`; observation
  is all tree log-probs plus final depth and sequence length; discount γ=0.9.
- Reward signal is cycle throughput: `L_A / (T_draft + T_verify)`.
- Training curriculum: (1) train size policy against random draft depths sampled
uniformly from [1, 12], (2) train depth policy against fixed `V = 60`, (3)
alternate to co-adapt. Two alternation rounds suffice per the paper.
- Results: 2.24×–4.32× speedup over vanilla AR decoding; up to +36.4% over Eagle3;
under 1.5% policy overhead on Llama.

### Our idea to beat LTD (idea3 — the latest version)

**Core claim:** LTD's two-policy split and its post-hoc width pruning are both
suboptimal. We can do better with a **single, lightweight MLP policy** that jointly
controls depth and width **during** tree construction.

**Architecture.** At every draft layer `m`, our MLP reads a richer state vector and
outputs a *tuple* `(f_m, c_m)` — two real numbers in `[0, 1]`:

- `**f_m` (frontier retention ratio)** — decides *which frontier parents to expand*
for this layer. Given the current frontier with cumulative path probabilities
`s_1, ..., s_n`, we normalize them into a distribution over the frontier, sort
descending by weight, and select the **smallest top-k set whose cumulative weight
is at least `f_m`**. Only those frontier parents get expanded. This is
nucleus-sampling–style cumulative-mass truncation, but applied to the *frontier*
rather than to a single token distribution, and governed by a learned policy
rather than a fixed threshold.
- `**c_m` (child-mass retention ratio)** — decides, for each expanded parent,
*how many of its children to keep*. Rank the parent's candidate children by next-
token probability; keep the smallest top-k set with cumulative probability mass
≥ `c_m`.

This gives us dynamic **width pruning during construction** (both across the frontier
and within each parent's children), not just after. When `f_m` is small, fewer paths
are expanded that layer, saving drafter forward passes. When `c_m` is small, each
expansion stays narrow. When both are near 1, the method reduces to a standard
beam search.

**Depth control is implicit.** Because `f_m` can select zero frontier nodes in the
limit (or because we can add a simple "stop if no expanded frontier" rule), we don't
need a separate depth policy. Growth halts naturally when the policy decides there
are no promising frontier paths left to extend.

**State vector.** Richer than LTD's: current draft depth, current context length,
budget/ratios allocated at previous layers, cumulative log-probabilities of frontier
paths, and next-token probability distributions. We will experiment with both MLP
and SSM policy heads (to see whether a sequential model over layers buys anything
over the per-layer MLP).

**Reward.** LTD uses `accepted_tokens / (T_draft + T_verify)`. We use
`accepted_tokens / T_total`, where `T_total` includes policy inference time. This
pushes the optimizer to care about the cost of its own calls, which is nontrivial
when the policy is wider (e.g., SSM) or called more often.

**Training.** LTD uses pure PPO (on-policy, sample-inefficient, required 1M depth-
policy steps and 100K size-policy steps). We plan to ablate three alternatives:

1. **Pure PPO** (LTD's recipe) — the baseline for fair comparison.
2. **Oracle-labeled imitation learning / DAgger.** Construct an oracle labeler by
  grid-searching or exhaustively evaluating `(f_m, c_m)` choices offline on cached
   draft trees; train the policy via supervised regression on the oracle's labels,
   then optionally DAgger-style correct distribution shift by rolling out the
   learned policy and relabeling.
3. **Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) with a continuous action.** Unlike LTD's discrete
  action space, our `(f_m, c_m)` is naturally continuous in `[0, 1]²`, which fits
   SAC's entropy-regularized continuous control regime. SAC is off-policy → far
   more sample-efficient than PPO.

**Expected wins over LTD:**

1. *Less wasted drafter compute* — we prune width *during* construction, not just
  at verification time.
2. *Smaller total policy footprint* — one MLP instead of two.
3. *Natural continuous-control formulation* — opens up off-policy methods that PPO
  forecloses on.
4. *Richer observation* — captures information LTD's depth-policy ablations
  suggested was missing (cumulative log-probs, allocation history, full next-token
   distributions).

**Plan for evaluation.** Reproduce LTD on Modal with Qwen3-14B and Qwen3-32B as
targets and their Eagle3 heads as drafters. Train on HumanEval; evaluate on
MT-Bench, GSM8K, Alpaca, and Natural Questions. Report end-to-end speedup vs.
vanilla AR and vs. LTD, plus average acceptance length τ per cycle.

Literature sweep: threats to the joint tree-width controller for speculative decoding

**Idea3's core claim — that a single MLP outputting continuous (f_m, c_m) retention ratios can jointly control tree depth and width during draft construction — faces no exact prior-art duplicate, but confronts serious threats from at least three directions.** C2T already deploys a learned classifier for per-node tree pruning during construction, directly pre-empting the "learned dynamic width" component. SAC's well-documented gradient saturation in bounded [0,1]² action spaces and the LLM inference community's obsession with eliminating even microsecond-level kernel launch overhead pose critical implementation risks. On the other hand, the single-policy joint-control design finds strong support in the co-adaptation literature: unified RL consistently outperforms modular alternatives when objectives align. ## Bucket A — Direct prior art on the core mechanism

These papers already implement some form of learned or dynamic tree-width control during draft construction. No paper replicates idea3's exact (f_m, c_m) dual-continuous-ratio mechanism, but two come close enough to qualify as high-severity prior art.

**1. C2T: A Classifier-Based Tree Construction Method in Speculative Decoding** Feiye Huo, Jianchao Tan, Kefeng Zhang, Xunliang Cai, Shengli Sun · arXiv:2502.13652 [Aussieai](https://www.aussieai.com/research/speculative-decoding) · Feb 2025 (submitted to ICLR 2026, withdrawn) **Bucket: A** · **Severity: Critical**

C2T trains a **2-layer FFN classifier with only 241 parameters** that takes three features (joint probability, entropy, depth) and outputs a confidence score per draft token, deciding whether to include it in the verification tree. [OpenReview](https://openreview.net/forum?id=8pX8He3Yyg) The classifier operates during tree construction, dynamically generating and pruning token trees layer by layer. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.13652) It reduces candidate tokens by **25%** while maintaining or improving acceptance length over EAGLE-2. [arXivarXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13652) The classifier is plug-and-play on top of EAGLE-2/3 and demonstrates cross-dataset transferability. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.13652)

**Clash with idea3:** C2T is the closest existing work to idea3's core mechanism — a learned controller making per-node expansion decisions during tree construction. However, C2T uses **binary include/exclude** classification rather than continuous (f_m, c_m) ratios, employs only 3 fixed features (vs. idea3's richer state vector), and does not use nucleus-style cumulative-mass truncation. Idea3's continuous control and richer state representation are the primary differentiators. Challenges: **frontier retention concept, dynamic width during construction, learned controller design**.

**Artifact:** Code not yet publicly released.

**Clash with idea3:** NDE uses a learned neural controller for dynamic during-construction tree expansion — the same architectural goal as idea3. ## Integrated threat assessment

The evidence organizes into three tiers of risk:

**Tier 1 — Implementation risks that could kill the project.** SAC's gradient saturation in bounded [0,1]² (GAC paper shows **46.4%** of samples affected) and per-layer policy overhead (when draft model forward passes are ~530μs, adding 160–320μs of policy calls is **30–60%** overhead) are the most dangerous threats. The mitigation path for SAC is clear: use GAC's geometric approach, PPO with clipped ratios, or Beta-distribution policies instead of Gaussian-tanh. For overhead, consider amortization (call the policy once per tree, not per layer) or fuse the MLP into the draft model's computation graph.

**Tier 2 — Prior art that narrows the novelty claim.** C2T and NDE already deploy learned controllers for dynamic tree construction. EAGLE-2, DySpec, TALON, and HeteroSpec demonstrate heuristic dynamic width. Idea3's specific novelty reduces to: (a) dual continuous [0,1] outputs vs. binary classification, (b) nucleus-style cumulative-mass truncation vs. threshold pruning, and (c) richer state representation. This narrowing requires idea3 to **demonstrate empirically** that continuous control and richer state outperform C2T's 241-parameter binary classifier — a high bar given C2T's simplicity.

**Tier 3 — The single-MLP design is well-supported.** Unlike the other threats, the co-adaptation literature consistently favors idea3's unified approach over LTD's modular design. Terry et al.'s theoretical result (centralization eliminates nonstationarity), CoRL's empirical finding (unified RL beats alternating), and the FARL analysis (factoring correlated actions introduces bias) all support a single MLP. However, the FARL paper also notes that independent factoring works well when dimensions are independent — if f_m and c_m turn out to be weakly correlated, LTD's split may be equivalent.

The strongest overall recommendation: idea3 should (1) replace SAC with PPO or a geometric alternative for the [0,1]² space, (2) benchmark aggressively against C2T and DySpec as the closest competitors, (3) address the overhead concern by profiling per-layer MLP cost against amortized alternatives, and (4) use CALM's conformal calibration framework to provide acceptance-rate guarantees for the continuous thresholds.

## C2T (arXiv:2502.13652, Huo et al., Meituan + Peking U, Feb 2025)

**What it actually does.** C2T runs *during* EAGLE-2's tree expansion. At every tree layer, after the draft model emits its next-layer candidates, EAGLE-2 would normally keep top-K by joint probability. C2T instead passes each candidate's `(joint_prob, entropy, depth)` into a 2-layer FFN sigmoid-classifier (`Cls`), gets a confidence score `C_i ∈ (0, 1)`, and keeps nodes with `C_i > β = 0.5`. Optional TopK cap on top of that. The classifier is trained *supervised* on offline accept/reject labels — no RL. 25% fewer verified tokens, same τ.

**Where it overlaps with idea3.** The *goal* is identical — a learned controller that prunes the tree during construction, plug-and-play on top of EAGLE-2/3. The architecture (tiny FFN on scalar features) is even simpler than what we'd propose.

**Where it is genuinely different from idea3.**

1. **Per-node vs per-layer.** C2T calls the classifier *once per candidate token*. For a layer with 150 candidates that's 150 forward passes through the classifier. Idea3's design calls its MLP *once per layer* and makes a single `(f_m, c_m)` decision that governs the whole frontier. This is actually a potential *win* for idea3 on overhead: fewer kernel launches. (C2T ducks the overhead problem by making the classifier absurdly tiny — 241 params — but this caps its representational capacity at almost-nothing.)
2. **Independent per-node threshold vs frontier-coupled ranking.** C2T decides each node independently by comparing its score to a fixed `β = 0.5`. If the draft model emits a layer where all 10 nodes score 0.52, C2T keeps all 10. If the next layer is "harder" and all 10 nodes score 0.48, C2T keeps none. Our `f_m` mechanism *normalizes across the frontier* — it always keeps *at least* the top-k nodes that together hold ≥ `f_m` of the cumulative mass, regardless of absolute confidence. This prevents both bimodal failure modes. **This is the strongest surviving novelty of idea3.**
3. **Supervised (C2T) vs RL (idea3).** C2T trains on offline (tree, accepted?) labels. The reward is implicit: "predict what got accepted." This is blind to throughput — a policy that accepts an enormous tree of certain tokens would score well under C2T's loss but terribly under LTD-style throughput reward. Idea3's RL reward directly optimizes throughput. This is a defensible contribution *if* we show empirically that the RL-trained policy captures a speed/quality tradeoff that C2T's supervised classifier misses.
4. **Ablation evidence from C2T's paper.** Their feature ablation (Table 1) shows `joint_prob` dominates; `entropy` and `depth` alone are "corrective." All three combined is meaningfully better. This suggests LTD's intuition (minimal features are fine) is only half right, and our plan to use richer features is backed by C2T's own ablation.

**Net assessment.** C2T narrows our novelty claim but does not preempt idea3's core mechanism. We must benchmark directly against C2T (their code is not public yet, but the paper is clear enough to reimplement the 2-layer FFN + training recipe in an afternoon).

## NDE / Dynamic Delayed Tree Expansion (arXiv:2602.16994, Thomas, Kitanovski, Goldblum, Pal; ICML 2026)

**What it actually does.** Distinct verification regime — NDE targets **OT-based** (optimal-transport) multi-path verification (SpecInfer-style), not EAGLE-3's traversal verification. It first shows systematically that Traversal Verification beats all OT variants under matched settings. Then it proposes **delayed expansion**: draft a single-path prefix first, then branch only at a learned branching point. A small MLP takes `(root_features, draft_pass_time, target_pass_time)` and picks 3 integers `(path_depth, expansion_factor, expansion_depth)` per cycle. This lets OT-based verification finally match Traversal, with +5% throughput.

**Where it overlaps with idea3.** Both are "learned neural selector for tree structure." Both use a small MLP. Both claim context-dependent tree decisions.

**Where it is genuinely different.**

1. **Verification regime.** NDE improves OT-based verification; we (and LTD) sit on Traversal Verification via EAGLE-3. The report's claim that NDE is Bucket A prior art is overstated — we're not in competition with NDE, we're in a different branch of the tree.
2. **Granularity of decision.** NDE picks 3 integers *once per decoding cycle*. Idea3 picks continuous `(f_m, c_m)` *once per draft layer*. NDE's policy cannot adapt mid-construction; ours can.
3. **Training signal.** NDE trains supervised on offline grid-search-derived optimal parameters, similar to C2T and dissimilar from LTD/us.

**Net assessment.** Not direct prior art for our specific problem. We should cite it and clearly demarcate the regime difference — "we operate on EAGLE-3 with traversal verification; NDE operates on SpecInfer-style OT verification."

## GAC (arXiv:2511.08234, Lin, Glasgow, Nov 2025)

**What it actually does.** Argues that SAC's standard "Gaussian + tanh-squash" action parameterization has a fundamental geometric mismatch with bounded action spaces: samples cluster near boundaries where tanh's gradient vanishes. They show ~40% of pre-squashed SAC samples on HalfCheetah are in low-gradient regions. Proposes replacing Gaussian+tanh with a direction vector on the unit sphere `S^{d−1}` plus a learned concentration scalar. 37.6% improvement over SAC on Ant-v4.

**Threat to idea3's SAC ablation.** Idea3 proposed SAC as a sample-efficient alternative to PPO for training the `(f_m, c_m)` policy, where actions live in `[0, 1]²`. GAC's finding is direct: SAC + tanh → near-boundary saturation → stuck policies. And the bounded action region in our case is *precisely* where bad behavior happens (e.g., `f_m = 0` = always stop, `f_m = 1` = never stop).

**Can GAC's fix be applied to our problem?** Partially. GAC requires a meaningful direction/magnitude decomposition (e.g., "which direction do I move the robot arm"). Idea3's `(f_m, c_m)` has no such geometric interpretation — they are two independent retention ratios. So GAC's specific spherical mechanism doesn't transfer. The mitigations that *do* transfer:

1. **Drop SAC, keep PPO.** PPO's clipped ratio objective doesn't suffer from the tanh-saturation issue in the same way (GAC explicitly notes this).
2. **Use Beta distributions.** For each of `f_m` and `c_m`, a Beta(α, β) distribution on `[0, 1]` is reparameterizable and natively bounded. Idea3 would emit `(α_f, β_f, α_c, β_c)` per layer.
3. **Discretize.** Bin `f_m` into 10 buckets, `c_m` into 10 buckets → 100 discrete actions. Use PPO categorical, exactly like LTD's discrete action space.

Mitigation (1) is trivially available — it's just "use LTD's training recipe." Mitigation (3) is the safest and also cheapest to implement. We should **drop the SAC ablation** entirely.

## cpu-inference-overhead (arXiv:2603.22774, Chung et al., Georgia Tech, Mar 2026)

**What it actually does.** Measures CPU-side bottlenecks in multi-GPU LLM inference. Key result: under CPU pressure, kernel launch latency escalates from μs to ms, and **CUDA Graphs cannot capture dynamic per-decode-step decisions** (EOS checks, dynamic control flow). So every dynamic per-step operation costs a fresh kernel launch. Reports that adequate CPU provisioning alone recovers 1.36–5.40× TTFT improvements.

**Threat to idea3's `T_total` reward framing.** The argument is: idea3 calls its MLP at every draft layer (8–12 times per cycle). Every call requires a kernel launch. Under CPU contention, each launch costs much more than the MLP forward pass itself. If the MLP forward is ~15μs and the launch is ~50μs, the MLP "overhead" is 4× the compute. BlockDrop's own ablation showed this: their sequential per-layer RL-gated ResNet was *slower* than the full net because of this exact issue.

**What this means for idea3.** Two things.

1. **Our `T_total` reward framing is *more* important, not less.** The reward must measure the whole cycle's wall-clock time (including our policy's calls + kernel launches), otherwise the optimizer will propose policies that look good on FLOPs but blow up on latency.
2. **We should benchmark against a "policy-fused draft model" variant.** Instead of calling the MLP externally, have the draft model's compute graph emit `(f_m, c_m)` as an extra head — one less kernel launch per layer. This is an engineering mitigation that does not change the RL problem.

For a single-GPU course project this concern is softened — we're not multi-GPU and our CPU is probably not pressured. But for the paper story, "we empirically measure per-layer policy overhead and find it is <X% of cycle time" is a reportable result that directly answers this threat.

## Novelty story in one paragraph

LTD's depth policy emits a single global CONTINUE/STOP bit per draft layer — the same decision is applied to every frontier node. This is a degenerate form of width control: all nodes continue together or stop together. We replace it with a **per-layer continuous frontier-mass policy `f_m` ∈ [0,1]**. At each layer, `f_m` selects the smallest top-k subset of frontier nodes whose cumulative path probability mass reaches at least `f_m`, and only those are expanded. This lets low-probability frontier paths be dropped early while high-probability ones keep expanding — true dynamic width during construction, not the global continue/stop switch LTD uses. We train with PPO against the same throughput reward as LTD, with `T_total` corrected to include policy inference time. C2T's per-node threshold classifier is our strongest existing competitor; we argue (and empirically show) that frontier-coupled nucleus-mass selection outperforms per-node thresholds because it is invariant to the absolute scale of confidence scores.
